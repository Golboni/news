Peace Talks Collapse in Islamabad After 21 Hours; Vance Leaves Without Deal

US and Iran hold first direct face-to-face negotiations in decades — nuclear enrichment remains the unbridgeable divide; ceasefire hangs by a thread as Strait still closed
Compiled from wire reports  ·  April 12, 2026

The most significant direct diplomatic engagement between Washington and Tehran in decades ended without agreement Sunday after more than 21 hours of talks in Islamabad, Pakistan. Vice President JD Vance, leading the US delegation alongside special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner, departed Pakistan empty-handed after Iran declined to commit to abandoning its nuclear program. "The bad news is that we have not reached an agreement," Vance told reporters before boarding Air Force Two. "And I think that's bad news for Iran much more than it's bad news for the US."

The talks — brokered by Pakistan, which has emerged as a surprising diplomatic linchpin — were described by Al Jazeera's correspondent as "neither a breakthrough nor a breakdown." Both sides agreed to keep talking through Pakistani intermediaries, and Pakistan's Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar urged both parties to uphold the fragile two-week ceasefire announced April 8th.

The core sticking point was stark: Washington demanded an affirmative commitment that Iran will not seek nuclear weapons or the tools to rapidly achieve them. Iran presented its own "red lines," including war reparations, release of frozen assets, and a halt to Israeli strikes on Hezbollah in Lebanon — a conflict the US insists falls outside the ceasefire framework.

Complicating the talks, Iran acknowledged it had lost track of mines it planted in the Strait of Hormuz, leaving it unable to fully open the waterway even if it wanted to. The US Navy began mine-clearing operations, with two destroyers transiting the strait — the first such passage since the war began. Iranian state media disputed the military's account.

"We're sweeping the strait. Whether we make a deal or not makes no difference to me." — President Trump, speaking to reporters during overnight talks

The war, now in its seventh week, has killed at least 3,000 people in Iran, over 2,000 in Lebanon, and 23 in Israel. Iran's grip on the strait has effectively severed Persian Gulf oil exports from global markets, sending crude above $94 a barrel even after the ceasefire announcement triggered a brief rally.

Israel pressed ahead with strikes in Lebanon, killing over 300 people on the day the ceasefire was announced — the deadliest single day in Lebanon since the war began. Netanyahu authorized direct negotiations with Beirut, with Israeli-Lebanese talks expected to begin Tuesday in Washington. Iran and Pakistan say Lebanon is included in any ceasefire; Israel and the US say it is not.

Pope Leo XIV, in his strongest statement yet, denounced what he called the "delusion of omnipotence" driving the conflict and called on all parties to halt fighting. Egyptian, Saudi, Qatari, and Chinese officials were present in Islamabad in indirect facilitation roles.

Pakistan will continue its mediation role in the days ahead. The two-week ceasefire clock is running. If it expires without a framework, analysts warn a return to active strikes is likely.

Markets & Economy

Ceasefire Bounce Fades as Hormuz Stays Shut

Brief surge on truce news reversed as Strait remains closed; oil stays above $94; food inflation fears mount globally

Markets surged when Trump announced the two-week ceasefire on April 8th — the S&P 500 rose 2.5%, the Dow jumped 1,325 points, and the Nasdaq gained 2.8%. South Korea's Kospi surged 6.9% and Germany's DAX rose 5.1%. But the gains have been tempered by the reality that the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with fewer than a dozen ships transiting since the ceasefire compared to over 100 per day before the war.

The IMF, World Bank, and World Food Programme jointly warned that the conflict has triggered a major energy shock that could drive up global food prices, particularly in low-income import-dependent nations. UK farm running costs are already 7% higher year-over-year; the Food and Drink Federation expects UK food inflation to hit at least 9% before year-end.

"The Strait of Hormuz is not open. Access is being restricted, conditioned, and controlled." — Sultan Al Jaber, UAE Industry Minister, April 9

Polymarket flagged suspicious trading in the days before Trump's ceasefire announcement, with one wallet turning a $72,000 bet into $200,000 profit and another wallet created just 12 minutes before the post netting an estimated $48,500.

Anthropic Unveils 'Project Glasswing' — Claude Mythos Finds Thousands of Zero-Days

April 10, 2026
