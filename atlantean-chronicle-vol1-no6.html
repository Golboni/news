U.S. Strikes Iran After Navy Destroyers Attacked in Strait of Hormuz

Washington launches retaliatory strikes on Iranian missile and drone sites after Tehran targets three U.S. Navy destroyers — NATO allies left in the dark, raising fears of alliance fracture

By The Chronicle Foreign Desk  ·  May 7–8, 2026

The United States carried out strikes on Iranian military sites Thursday after Iran launched missiles, drones, and small boats against three U.S. Navy destroyers operating in the Strait of Hormuz. U.S. Central Command confirmed that all inbound threats were eliminated before the retaliatory strikes targeted Iranian missile and drone launch facilities. Iran accused the United States of first violating a ceasefire by firing on an Iranian oil tanker heading toward the strait, a claim Washington denied.

The exchange marks the most direct military confrontation between the two countries since the Strait closure began in February. President Trump convened his national security team and, according to multiple sources, did not consult NATO allies before ordering the strikes. The decision has inflamed tensions across the alliance and is prompting urgent discussions about rethinking U.S. leadership within NATO going forward.

The conflict's economic toll continues to deepen. WTI crude settled at $94.81 on Thursday with Brent crossing $100, as the prolonged Strait closure has now been described by the International Energy Agency as the largest oil supply disruption in history, surpassing the 1970s oil shocks. Baker Hughes projects no full reopening before the second half of 2026.

In a parallel diplomatic development, the MV Hondius hantavirus situation has drawn WHO Director General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus into a direct appeal to the Trump administration, hoping the outbreak will prompt broader public health engagement. Spanish authorities are now preparing to receive the vessel's 140+ passengers as it diverts to the Canary Islands after Cape Verde refused port entry.

Hantavirus Outbreak Strands 149 Aboard MV Hondius in Atlantic

Three passengers dead, one critically ill in South Africa — the vessel originated in Argentina, where the Andes strain of hantavirus is endemic and capable of limited human-to-human transmission.

By The Chronicle Foreign Desk · May 5–6, 2026

A hantavirus cluster aboard the Dutch-flagged expedition vessel MV Hondius has killed three passengers and left one critically ill in a Johannesburg ICU, with the ship currently anchored off Praia, the capital of Cape Verde, after being refused entry to port. The World Health Organization confirmed two laboratory-positive cases of hantavirus as of May 4, with five additional suspected cases under investigation across 23 nationalities.

The MV Hondius departed Ushuaia, Argentina on April 1 on a remote Atlantic expedition routing through Antarctica, Saint Helena, and Ascension Island before the first illnesses were reported mid-April. The current investigative hypothesis is that some passengers were exposed to Andes virus (ANDV) during excursions in Argentina before boarding — and may have subsequently transmitted the virus to fellow passengers in the confined setting of the ship.

Andes virus is uniquely dangerous among the hantavirus family: it is the only known strain with documented human-to-human transmission, primarily through close household or patient contact. The CDC places the fatality rate for hantavirus pulmonary syndrome at approximately 38% once respiratory symptoms develop. There is no approved antiviral treatment.

WHO's regional director for Europe stated there is no need for broader travel restrictions, and the risk to the general public remains low. Oceanwide Expeditions is considering sailing the vessel to Spain as port access in Cape Verde remains unresolved.


Health Briefing

What Is Hantavirus — And Why the Andes Strain Matters

Hantaviruses are rodent-borne zoonotic diseases typically contracted through contact with infected rodent waste. Most strains cannot spread between people — with one exception. The Andes virus, endemic to Chile and Argentina, is the only hantavirus strain with confirmed human-to-human transmission, making the MV Hondius outbreak uniquely concerning for travel medicine. Fatigue, fever, and rapid progression to acute respiratory distress define the clinical course. No antiviral treatment exists; supportive care and early oxygen are the primary interventions.



Location · Context

Cape Verde: Strategic Crossroads Becomes Outbreak Flashpoint

The Cabo Verde archipelago — a Portuguese-speaking island nation 570 km off West Africa's coast — is a growing tourism hub, hosting roughly 900,000 visitors annually. Its position as a maritime waypoint in the mid-Atlantic makes it a routine stop for expedition vessels and transatlantic crossings alike. The port authority's refusal to grant MV Hondius entry follows international health protocols for vessels with active outbreak notifications. Three suspected cases were separately evacuated to the Netherlands for further care, complicating the multi-country contact tracing effort now underway.



Traveler Advisory

Expedition & Remote Travel: What Passengers Should Know

Expedition voyages to remote wildlife zones — Antarctica, Patagonia, sub-Antarctic islands — carry elevated exposure risk for zoonotic disease not present on mainstream cruise itineraries. Passengers who visited rural Argentina, Chile, or similar hantavirus-endemic areas and develop fever, fatigue, or respiratory symptoms within four weeks should seek immediate evaluation and disclose their travel history. The WHO emphasizes that standard cruise travel carries no elevated hantavirus risk — the MV Hondius case is tied to the vessel's specific South American departure point and wildlife excursion activities.

Anthropic Taps Colossus —
SpaceX Supercomputer Now Powers Claude

In a deal that rewrites the AI compute playbook, Anthropic has secured all capacity at SpaceX's Colossus 1 data center — instantly doubling Claude Code limits and raising Opus API rate limits for paid subscribers worldwide.

By The Chronicle Tech Desk  ·  May 6–8, 2026  ·  Sources: Anthropic, CNBC, Bloomberg, CoinDesk
220K+ NVIDIA GPUs
300MW New Capacity
2× Claude Code Limits
5GW Amazon Deal
$50B Fluidstack Infra

Anthropic announced Wednesday it had signed an agreement with SpaceX to access all compute capacity at Colossus 1, the massive AI supercomputer cluster located in Memphis, Tennessee. The deal delivers more than 300 megawatts of power and over 220,000 NVIDIA GPUs — including dense deployments of H100, H200, and next-generation GB200 accelerators — within the month, representing one of the largest single compute acquisitions in AI history.

The agreement immediately triggered tangible upgrades for Claude users. Claude Code's five-hour rate limits were doubled for Pro, Max, Team, and Enterprise subscribers, effective the same day. Peak-hour slowdowns that had plagued Claude Pro and Max users during high-traffic periods were removed. Opus API rate limits were raised significantly, addressing a persistent bottleneck for enterprise developers building on Claude's most capable model tier.

The Colossus 1 deal is the capstone of a sweeping infrastructure buildout that Anthropic has been quietly assembling. The company has previously signed a 5-gigawatt agreement with Amazon — including nearly 1 gigawatt of new capacity arriving by year-end — a 5-gigawatt deal with Google and Broadcom coming online in 2027, a Microsoft-NVIDIA strategic partnership covering $30 billion of Azure capacity, and a $50 billion U.S. AI infrastructure investment with Fluidstack.

The deal carries unusual political texture. SpaceX merged with Elon Musk's xAI earlier this year in an all-stock deal valuing xAI at approximately $250 billion, bringing Colossus infrastructure under the rocket company's umbrella as SpaceXAI. Musk has repeatedly criticized Anthropic, calling it "misanthropic," yet said discussions with senior Anthropic staff shaped his view of the company's approach to responsible AI development. Anthropic will use Colossus 1 for Claude inference, while SpaceXAI trains future Grok models on the newer Colossus 2 cluster.

Perhaps most striking is what lies beyond the deal's immediate scope. Anthropic has expressed interest in partnering with SpaceX to develop multiple gigawatts of orbital AI compute capacity — a signal that the race for AI infrastructure is beginning to outpace what terrestrial power grids, land availability, and cooling systems can sustainably deliver.
