WHO Declares Global Emergency as Ebola Bundibugyo Tears Through Central Africa

A fast-moving outbreak of a rare Ebola strain in the Democratic Republic of Congo has killed 216 and infected nearly 1,000 — prompting the World Health Organization to invoke its highest alert level for the first time since 2022, while triggering emergency screening at Atlanta's Hartsfield-Jackson International Airport.

By The Chronicle World Desk  ·  May 22–24, 2026

The World Health Organization declared the Ebola Bundibugyo outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo a Public Health Emergency of International Concern on May 17, 2026 — the most powerful alarm the agency can sound. As of May 23, confirmed and suspected cases had reached 968, with at least 216 deaths concentrated in Ituri, Nord-Kivu, and Sud-Kivu provinces in eastern DRC. Five linked cases were subsequently confirmed in Uganda's capital, Kampala, raising immediate fears of urban spread in a densely populated regional hub with extensive international air connections.

The causative pathogen — Orthoebolavirus bundibugyoense, the Bundibugyo strain — is genetically distinct from the Zaire Ebola virus responsible for the catastrophic 2014–16 West African epidemic. The critical distinction, WHO's Emergency Committee emphasized in its May 22 temporary recommendations, is that no approved vaccines or specific antiviral treatments currently exist for the Bundibugyo variant. All international vaccine stockpiles target the Zaire strain. Accelerated clinical trials of experimental ring vaccination compounds are now underway in Ituri Province, but efficacy results are expected to take months.

The international response has been severely complicated by ongoing armed conflict in eastern DRC between government forces and the M23 rebel group. Epidemiologists and contact tracers from WHO, MSF, and the Africa CDC face access constraints in Ituri Province, where insecurity has interrupted supply chains and forced repeated evacuation of response teams from field sites. WHO characterizes the operational environment as representing "a convergence of worst-case factors" — conflict, porous international borders, absent vaccine coverage, and a naive regional population with no prior exposure to the Bundibugyo strain.

The United States CDC, acting with DHS, activated enhanced entry screening at three major international airports effective May 22. Travelers arriving from or transiting through DRC, Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi, or South Sudan within the previous 21 days are subject to temperature screening, health questionnaire review, and potential isolation. Hartsfield-Jackson Atlanta — the world's busiest airport — joined Dulles International and George Bush Intercontinental Houston in the initial screening activation sequence. The ATL designation carries particular significance given the airport's role as the primary international gateway for the southeastern U.S. business corridor and its direct-connection flight paths through Addis Ababa into East Africa.

Trump Signals Unfinalized Framework for Iran Nuclear Deal

World Desk  ·  May 25, 2026

President Trump announced that U.S. and Israeli officials have reached the outline of an unfinalized framework addressing Iran's nuclear program, with a two-month negotiation window envisioned. Secretary of State Rubio arrived in India for a Quad meeting — observers link the talks to multilateral pressure on Tehran ahead of any formal signing ceremony. No binding agreement has been signed.

EU AI Act Moves From Guidance to Binding Obligation

World Desk  ·  May 24, 2026

The European Union's AI Act — the world's first comprehensive legal framework governing artificial intelligence systems — has entered its first binding enforcement phase. High-risk AI deployments in critical infrastructure, education, employment, and law enforcement must now be registered in the EU AI database. Firms operating in the EU face fines of up to 3% of global annual revenue for non-compliance. Brussels has named 12 initial companies under enhanced supervisory review.

UN Condemns Taliban Child Marriage Decree

World Desk  ·  May 23, 2026

The United Nations expressed "grave concern" following the Taliban government's issuance of a new statutory framework on marriage and separation that includes provisions codifying child marriage under Islamic jurisprudence. The decree makes Afghanistan the only UN member state with an explicit statutory mechanism permitting the practice without age restriction. Human rights organizations and Western governments have formally condemned the law; the Taliban has dismissed objections as interference in domestic legal affairs.

Kevin Warsh Takes the Oath as Fed Chair — A New Monetary Era Begins

Economy  ·  May 24, 2026

Kevin Warsh was sworn in as the 17th Chair of the Federal Reserve on May 24, completing a confirmation process that cleared the Senate Banking Committee 12–9 along largely party lines. Warsh, who served on the Fed Board of Governors from 2006 to 2011 and played a central role in the 2008 crisis response, is a market-oriented institutionalist with a preference for rules-based policy frameworks over discretionary forward guidance. Markets absorbed the transition calmly — the S&P 500 extended its winning streak to eight consecutive weeks, the longest since December 2023, gaining 0.4% on Friday. The Dow added 294 points, briefly touching an intraday record. Warsh's first FOMC meeting is expected in mid-June, with markets pricing two 25-basis-point rate cuts before year-end.

Atlanta's Airport Goes to Elevated Health Screening — Ebola Protocols Live at Hartsfield-Jackson

The world's busiest airport activated CDC-mandated Ebola entry screening protocols as of May 22 — directly affecting Metro Atlanta travelers and Atlantis ITS clients transiting East Africa.

By The Chronicle Travel Desk  ·  May 22–24, 2026

What happened. The WHO declared an Ebola PHEIC on May 17 following confirmed urban spread to Kampala, Uganda. With Ethiopian Airlines maintaining a direct Atlanta–Addis Ababa route and secondary connections onward to Entebbe and Nairobi, CDC identified Hartsfield-Jackson as a meaningful exposure corridor. The ATL activation joins previously activated measures at Washington Dulles and Houston Intercontinental.

Who is affected. Travelers arriving on flights originating from or connecting through DRC, Uganda, Rwanda, Burundi, or South Sudan within the preceding 21 days. All other domestic and international passengers are unaffected. Business travelers on the Atlanta–Addis–Nairobi corridor should budget an additional 30–60 minutes for arrival processing for CDC health declaration completion and temperature screening.

What travelers should do. Retain all boarding passes and transit documentation showing routing. Complete the CDC health questionnaire completely and accurately. If you have traveled to DRC or Uganda within 21 days and develop fever, headache, muscle pain, or unexplained bleeding, contact a healthcare provider by telephone before presenting in person at any clinical facility, and disclose your travel history.

Atlantis ITS client impact. Any staff or client with international travel routing through East Africa should be briefed on the CDC advisory before departure. Atlantis recommends flagging affected itineraries to HR or operations management prior to travel. The Atlantis Travel Advisory Index for the East Africa region is elevated to AMBER through at least June 7, 2026.
