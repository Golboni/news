Switzerland to Open Secret Files on Auschwitz 'Angel of Death' Mengele

The Swiss Federal Intelligence Service will finally unseal long-classified files on Nazi war criminal Josef Mengele — decades after historians first requested access and amid persistent rumours he spent time in Switzerland while a global arrest warrant was active

By The Chronicle Foreign Desk  ·  May 16, 2026 · BBC News

Switzerland's Federal Intelligence Service announced this week that it will open long-sealed files on Josef Mengele, the Nazi doctor known as the Angel of Death, though the agency declined to say when the documents would be made available to historians and the public. The announcement ends decades of refusals that had frustrated researchers, war crimes investigators, and Holocaust scholars repeatedly seeking access to the classified material.

Mengele served as a doctor with Germany's Waffen SS and was posted to the Auschwitz extermination camp in Nazi-occupied Poland, where he personally selected prisoners for the gas chambers. An estimated 1.1 million people died at Auschwitz, including approximately one million Jews. Mengele also selected prisoners — primarily children and twins — for sadistic medical experiments before sending them to their deaths. His methodical cruelty on the camp's selection ramp earned him the name that has defined his historical infamy.

After Germany's defeat in 1945, Mengele quickly shed both his SS uniform and his name. Using a false identity, he obtained Red Cross travel documents at the Swiss consulate in Genoa, Italy — the same humanitarian travel documents intended for the millions of Europeans displaced or made stateless by the war. Mengele and other high-ranking Nazis were among those who exploited the system to obtain the papers. The Red Cross has since issued formal apologies for the failure that allowed war criminals to use its documentation infrastructure as an escape route to South America.

The rumours that Mengele spent time in Switzerland while an international arrest warrant was outstanding have circulated for decades. The Swiss government's sustained refusal to open its intelligence files fuelled suspicion that official Swiss records might document knowledge of Mengele's presence or movements — and perhaps explain why no action was taken. Historians have now pressed for answers across multiple political generations. The intelligence service's announcement that the files will be opened, even without a firm timeline, marks the first significant concession from Swiss authorities on the matter since the war's end.

Mengele ultimately lived out the remainder of his life in South America, dying in Brazil in 1979. He was never tried for war crimes. The opening of the Swiss files — whenever it occurs — may finally answer whether Swiss authorities had information that could have led to his capture, and chose to act on none of it.
